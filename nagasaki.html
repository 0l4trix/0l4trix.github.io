Nagasaki

Nagasaki today is a city that embodies a unique blend of history, culture, and resilience. Known for its tragic past as the second city to suffer an atomic bombing during World War II, Nagasaki has transformed into a beacon of peace and reconciliation. The city's picturesque landscapes, vibrant festivals, and diverse culinary scene showcase its rich heritage and unwavering spirit. Nagasaki's commitment to promoting peace and understanding serves as a testament to the enduring strength of its people and the power of hope in the face of adversity.

One of the most prominent memorials in Nagasaki is the Nagasaki Peace Park, which includes several monuments and structures contributed by various nations to honor the memory of those who lost their lives in the bombing. Nagasaki Peace Park is built on a low hill to the north of the hypocenter of the atomic bomb blast. It was created to represent the wish for world peace and a vow that such a tragic war would never be repeated. Every year on August 9, the anniversary of the atomic bombing, a peace memorial ceremony is conducted in front of the Peace Statue and a peace declaration is made to the people of the world.

The Peace Statue

The Peace Statue, also known as the "Statue of Peace," stands tall at the center of the park, symbolizing the city's commitment to peace and nuclear disarmament. The statue represents a mixture of western and eastern art, religion, and ideology. It depicts a man with one arm raised towards the sky, symbolizing a call for peace and the prevention of future nuclear tragedies. The other arm is extended horizontally, representing the wish for harmony and reconciliation among all people.

The Fountain of Peace

The Peace Park also features the Fountain of Peace, constructed as a remembrance of the many atomic bomb victims who died searching for water. The fountain sprays the water upward in the form of two wings, evoking images of the dove of peace descending. Lines from a poem by a girl named Sachiko Yamaguchi, who was nine at the time of the bombing, are carved on a black stone plaque in front of the fountain. It reads: "I was thirsty beyond endurance. There was something oily on the surface of the water, but I wanted water so badly that I drank it just as it was."

The Nagasaki Atomic Bomb Museum

The Nagasaki Atomic Bomb Museum, similarly to the Peace Memorial Museum in Hiroshima, is a must-visit destination for tourists seeking to learn about the tragic events that unfolded during World War II. The museum is dedicated to preserving the memory of the atomic bombing of Nagasaki on August 9, 1945, and educating visitors about the devastating impact of nuclear weapons. As you explore the museum, you will encounter powerful exhibits, artifacts, and firsthand accounts that provide a sobering look at the destruction caused by the bomb and the resilience of the people of Nagasaki in its aftermath.

The Hypocenter Park

The Hypocenter Park marks the exact spot where the atomic bomb "Fat Man" detonated over Nagasaki on 9 August 1945, serving as a solemn reminder of the destruction caused by nuclear weapons. You can see the ground that was irradiated and the remains of the destroyed Urakami Cathedral in their original state from that day. Now the area is lush with trees that began growing months after the blast, like phoenixes from the ashes. The park is a favorite place in spring to relax as 500 cherry trees blossom.
